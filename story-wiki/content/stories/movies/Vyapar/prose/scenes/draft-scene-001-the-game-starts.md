# Scene 001 — The Game Starts

**Location:** A flat in Gurugram. Saturday night. Diwali week — outside there are firecrackers, outside it smells like sulphur and festival. Inside: a folding table, a Monopoly board — but not the normal Monopoly board. This one is handmade. The properties have real names. The money denominations are wrong. Nobody noticed when the game arrived.
**Characters:** Aman (26, sales job, has been trying to buy a flat for two years), Priya (25, graphic designer, Aman's girlfriend), Kabir (28, Aman's best friend since school, the one who brought the game)
**Purpose:** The game begins. Nobody knows it's real yet. But the rules are already different from the rules they think they're playing by.

---

Kabir arrived with biryani and the game under one arm.

"Where'd you get this?" Aman said, turning the box over. The box looked normal — *Vyapar* on the front, in the right font, like every other version of the game he'd seen at relatives' houses. But the cardboard was heavier than it should have been, and when he shook it, the pieces moved with a weight that standard plastic tokens don't have.

"Some old shop near Palika Bazaar. The guy was selling it for fifty rupees. I thought it'd be a laugh."

"You thought an unknown board game from an old shop would be a laugh," Priya said, already serving herself rice.

"It's Monopoly. How different can it be?"

They set it up while they ate. The board opened flat and clean, and the first thing Aman noticed was that the property names weren't cities — they were local, specific. *Sector 12 Plot B.* *Old DLF Commercial.* *Basement Floor, Laxmi Nagar.* Real places, places that existed. He'd driven past two of them.

"Weird," he said.

"Atmospheric," Kabir said, and rolled the dice.

The rules were standard — or almost standard. There was one addition, printed on a small card tucked under the banker's tray: *In this game, property acquired through any means remains the player's regardless of game outcome. The board is the contract. What is taken is taken.*

Priya read it twice. "What does that mean?"

"Marketing copy," Kabir said. "To make it feel more dramatic. Pass me the banker's tray."

Aman read it again. *The board is the contract.* He put the card down.

They played for an hour and a half, and it was fun — the kind of fun that a biryani and Diwali and a slightly too-competitive friend group generates naturally. Kabir bought the commercial properties aggressively. Aman tried for the corner plots. Priya ran the railway stations with cheerful indifference.

Around ten o'clock, something shifted.

It was small: Kabir landed on a property Aman owned and, instead of paying rent, said, "What if I buy this off you? I'll give you double."

"That's not how the game works."

"In this game it might be." Kabir flipped the rules card over — on the back, which they hadn't looked at: *Direct transfer between players permitted at any agreed price. All agreements final.*

Priya frowned. "That's not standard."

"The whole game isn't standard." Kabir looked at Aman. "Double what you paid. Two hundred."

"It's pretend money," Priya said.

"Double," Kabir said, looking at Aman.

Aman looked at the board. At Kabir. At the specific quality of interest in Kabir's face — not competitive-friend interest, but something with a different texture. Focused. Patient.

"No," Aman said.

Something moved in Kabir's expression. Just a flicker.

"Fair enough," he said, and rolled again.

The game continued.

*Later — Priya asleep, Kabir gone home, Aman stacking the board back into its box — he found the other card. Not in the banker's tray. Under the board itself, pressed flat, as if it had always been there. On it was a list of seven names.*

*His name was fourth.*

*He didn't know any of the others. He stood in the kitchen with the card in his hand and the distant sound of the last of Diwali's firecrackers outside, and he felt the particular discomfort of a man who has just realized that the game he thought he started is a game that started long before he sat down.*

---

**Consequence:** The game is not a game. Aman doesn't know this yet — he tells himself the card is a manufacturing error, someone's prank, and puts it in his wallet for no clear reason. Kabir leaves that night with two of the game's secondary property cards in his jacket pocket. This is not noticed. It should have been. The board, under the box, holds the shape of what was played tonight. It will not reset.
