# Scene 001 — The First Rift

**Format:** Silent. No words are spoken. No names are given. Everything is carried by what is seen.
**Location:** His inner world. Then the space outside it. Then the edge of something new.
**Character:** He. One person. The planet below is him.
**Purpose:** He discovers that the space outside his world is not empty. He finds the first rift. He does not go through. He stands at its edge and understands — without words, the way you understand things that are too large for words — that his space has changed.

---

The planet is not young.

You can tell from orbit — from the particular density of what has accumulated there. The surface is layered. Things were built here over a long time, alone, by hand. In the northern hemisphere: a system of towers connected by walkways at different heights, each one slightly different from the last, as if the same problem was solved repeatedly and never quite to satisfaction. The towers are dark at the top but lit from within — the kind of structure that was built to be functional and became beautiful as a side effect of being very precisely functional.

The southern hemisphere is quieter. Flat, mostly. A wide plain of compressed sediment — the material of things that were processed and set down, not discarded, not forgotten, simply placed. The plain is not empty: there are objects arranged across it at irregular intervals, each one stable, each one left where it stopped being needed. They are organized, but the organization is private — a logic that belongs to whoever arranged them and would not be immediately legible to anyone else.

The weather moves in from the east.

There are storms here, but they are deliberate storms — the kind of atmospheric event that has been cycled through many times and is now understood, if not comfortable. They arrive, they move across the towers, they pass. The lightning hits the same places it always hits. The towers were built to take it.

The ocean — on the western edge, where the land finally gives out — is the one part of the planet that was not designed. It arrived. It is the oldest thing here. Its colour is not blue: it is the specific dark that comes from water that has been in one place long enough to hold the memory of everything that has moved through it. He does not go to the ocean often. But it is always there, at the edge of his awareness, present the way something present is always present even when unobserved.

---

He is standing at the outermost tower when it happens.

He has been there for some time — not resting, not working, just standing in the way a person stands when they have run out of the next thing to do and have not yet found the thing after that. The storm passed an hour ago. The air is very still. From here he can see most of the planet: the plain, the objects arranged across it, the ocean catching something distant.

He steps off the walkway.

He does not fall.

The rising is slow. Not dramatic — it happens the way a breath happens, as part of a larger rhythm he was always participating in and simply hadn't noticed. He rises. Below him, the planet organizes itself into a whole. He can see, from here, the thing he cannot see from inside: the shape. The pattern that all the towers and the plain and the ocean make together, viewed from outside. It is a specific shape. It is unmistakably his.

The planet is very small from here.

He rises until the planet is a thing he can hold in his field of vision without moving his gaze. He stops rising.

Around him: space. Dark. Complete. The kind of complete that is not threatening but is also not comfortable — the complete of a room with no furniture yet, waiting for decisions about what it should contain.

He holds still in it for a long time.

---

Then he sees the rift.

It is not close. It is not far. It is positioned in the space between those two things, at an angle from his planet that he does not have a name for because he has never had to name angles in his own space before. He would not have seen it immediately. But the storm cleared something — some residue, some atmospheric thickness — and in that clarity it is visible.

It is not a hole.

Or it is a hole, but not the way a hole in something solid is a hole. It is more like: a place where the dark is slightly different. A seam. As if the space in that location was once two separate pieces, and something pressed them together, and they held — and now the pressing is finished, and the seam remains.

The edges of it are warm.

He knows this before he reaches it, the way you know the temperature of a room before you are all the way through the door. Not heat — warmth. The specific warmth of something that has been near something else for long enough that the nearness left a mark.

He moves toward it.

At the edge, he stops.

Through the seam: another world. Not his colours. Not his architecture. Not his weather. Something else entirely — he cannot take it all in at once, not because it is overwhelming, but because it is *foreign* in the way that makes you slow down, the way you slow down in a place where you don't know the language and you want to be careful about what you miss.

What he can see: warmth in the palette, where his runs cool. Something growing, where his is built. The movement of things, where his stands still.

He does not go through.

He stands at the seam for a long time, looking at a world that is nothing like his, that exists in the space just outside his own space, that he did not know was there until this moment.

He does not go through.

But he raises one hand — slowly, without deciding to — and holds it near the edge of the seam. Not touching. Near.

The warmth intensifies by the smallest possible degree.

On the other side: nothing visible changes. But if you were watching from very far away, you might notice something — a slight adjustment in the weather of that other planet. A shift in the quality of the light. So small as to be almost nothing.

Almost.

---

He returns to his planet.

He descends. The towers receive him. The air smells the way it always smells — compressed sediment, the trace of the storm, the distant ocean. The walkways are where he left them. The objects on the plain are where he arranged them.

Everything is the same.

Except that he knows, now, that the space outside his world contains something that was not there before.

Or: that has always been there, and he simply could not see it until the storm cleared.

He goes to the outermost tower and stands where he stood before. Looks out at the plain. Looks at the sky.

The sky looks the same.

He looks at it for a long time anyway.

---

**Consequence:** The rift does not close. In the days that follow — measured in the way time is measured in an inner world, which is not by clocks but by the number of storms, the movement of the ocean, the gradual settling of the objects on the plain — it remains. Visible from orbit. Sometimes, from the outermost tower, he thinks he can feel the edge of it in the quality of the air — that slight differential warmth, arriving from one direction. He has not gone through. He has not decided not to. He is doing what the towers were built for: taking the weight of the thing, holding it, waiting to understand it before the next decision.
