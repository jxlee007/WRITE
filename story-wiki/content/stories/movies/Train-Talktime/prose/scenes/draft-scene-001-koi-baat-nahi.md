# Scene 001 — Koi Baat Nahi

*Episode 1 — "The Women on the 4:40"*

**Location:** The Shatabdi Express. Compartment B7, two facing seats by the window. The train is moving — small towns flashing past, the particular rhythm of rails that becomes invisible after the first ten minutes. It is 5:20 in the afternoon.
**Characters:** Surinder Kaur (63, Amritsar, coming back from a funeral, gold churiyaan, carries biscuits everywhere), Yasmin Bano (59, Lucknow, going to visit her son, was widowed six months ago, hasn't told the son how bad things are)
**Purpose:** Two aunties who would not have chosen to talk to each other talk. A corrupt death — Yasmin's husband, the subject of a government inquiry that went nowhere — becomes the ground on which two women who are supposed to be on opposite sides of an invisible line find that the line doesn't exist on trains.

---

Surinder had the window seat, which was correct because she had boarded first, and also because she had very specific feelings about windows on trains that were non-negotiable.

Yasmin sat across from her, arranged her dupatta, and looked at the seat pocket, and the window, and the seat pocket again. She did not look at Surinder. Surinder did not look at her.

The train moved.

After approximately three minutes, Surinder opened her bag and produced a packet of glucose biscuits, because she always had glucose biscuits, because you never knew, and she held the packet across the aisle.

"Biscuit?"

Yasmin looked at the packet. Then at Surinder. Then she took two biscuits. "Shukriya."

"Ji haan." Surinder took three for herself. "You've come from where?"

"Delhi."

"Going to?"

"Varanasi. My son is there."

"Ah." Surinder nodded. "First time visiting?"

"No. But the first time since—" Yasmin stopped. Started again. "Since my husband passed."

The train went through a shadow — a bridge or a cutting, the light briefly gone. When it came back, Surinder was looking at her with the attention of a woman who has been to enough funerals to recognize grief in its travel clothes.

"How long back?" she said.

"Six months."

"Ji." Surinder turned back to the window. "I just came from a funeral myself. My husband's cousin's son. Thirty-nine years old." She shook her head. "These things happen."

"Mine was fifty-eight." Yasmin's voice was level. "They say it was a heart attack."

Something in the way she said *they say* made Surinder turn back.

"What do you say?" she said.

Yasmin looked at her for a moment. She was a precise, private woman — a woman who had spent decades being careful about what she said and to whom. But there was something about this particular seat on this particular train, the specific anonymity of transit, that loosened things.

"He was under investigation," Yasmin said. "By the department. He was a government doctor — thirty years. They said there were irregularities in a procurement contract. He had nothing to do with it — the contract was signed before he even joined that department — but the inquiry started, and the papers wrote about it, and six months later—" she pressed her lips together — "his heart."

Surinder looked at her for a long time.

"The inquiry is closed?" she said.

"The inquiry is closed." Yasmin's voice was completely neutral. "They closed it on the grounds that the main person concerned was no longer available for questioning."

A silence.

"My son thinks it was the stress," Yasmin said. "He has made peace with this. I—" she stopped.

"You haven't," Surinder said.

"I cannot find the paperwork. His personal files. Every time I ask, the office says they're processing. Six months of processing." She looked at the window. "He was a good doctor. He used to stay past midnight writing referrals for poor patients so they wouldn't have to pay the specialist fees. He was not a corrupt man."

Surinder was quiet. Outside the window, a small station went past — a platform, two men with chai, a dog.

"My brother-in-law," Surinder said eventually. "Twenty years ago. He ran a school in Ludhiana. Very small, but his own. He reported a contractor who was siphoning building funds to the municipality. Two weeks later, there was a fire." She paused. "They said electrical fault."

Yasmin looked at her.

"These things," Surinder said, "they happen to everyone. In every house. We just don't talk about it because—" she gestured at the air, vaguely — "because who do you talk to."

"No one talks to each other," Yasmin said. "We all stay in our own—"

"Our own compartments." Surinder looked slightly amused by her own word choice. "Yes."

The train rumbled on. The light outside was going that particular orange that only exists on north Indian afternoons in summer — everything warm, everything slightly hazy.

Yasmin took another biscuit. Held it. "Your name?"

"Surinder. And yours?"

"Yasmin."

A small nod from each woman. An adjustment. The invisible inventory of a first meeting being quietly revised.

"Your son," Surinder said. "He's in Varanasi doing what?"

"Software. He's good at it. He wanted me to move there — after." Yasmin turned the biscuit over in her fingers. "I'm going to tell him I'll think about it."

"But you won't."

"Not yet." She ate the biscuit. "The house still has my husband's things. I'm not finished with them."

Surinder nodded. "You don't have to be finished."

"Everyone says I should be by now."

"Everyone," Surinder said, with cheerful authority, "is wrong."

---

**Consequence:** By Varanasi, Surinder has given Yasmin a contact — a retired journalist she knows who has written about procurement corruption before. Not a promise. Not a solution. Just a name and a number on the back of a biscuit wrapper. Yasmin takes it not because she believes it will lead anywhere, but because someone believed her husband. That is enough, for now, to arrive at her son's door without her face doing what it has been doing for six months.
