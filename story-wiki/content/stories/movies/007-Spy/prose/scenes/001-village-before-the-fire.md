# Scene 001 — Village Before the Fire

**Location:** Rural India — a small village in the hills, unnamed deliberately  
**Characters:** Young Alpha (no name given), unnamed villagers, glimpses of a father  
**Purpose:** Establish the world Alpha loses. Root him in something warm before it is taken. Seed the detachment that will define him — even here, he is slightly apart from it all.

---

The mornings came quietly in the hills.

No alarm, no urgency — just the slow brightening through the gaps in the kaccha wall, the smell of woodsmoke from the neighbour's kitchen drifting in. He would lie still for a moment after waking, listening. Cattle moving. A woman calling her child. The distant, rhythmic knock of someone splitting wood.

He was twelve. He would not know until much later that these sounds had a weight — that they were the kind of sounds you could carry for the rest of your life, the way you carry something without knowing you are carrying it at all.

His father had a way of naming things precisely.

*That bird is a drongo. See the tail — forked, like a tuning fork. It will chase anything out of its territory, even a hawk.*

The boy had watched the small dark bird dive at a kite three times its size, screaming at it, furious, relentless, until the kite simply left. He had asked his father why it bothered. The kite would come back.

*Because the territory is all it has,* his father had said. *If it stops defending it, it no longer exists.*

He had not understood it then. He filed it away, the way he filed most things — in some quiet interior room he did not have a name for.

---

The village had a rhythm. He knew it the way he knew his own heartbeat — not by thinking about it, but by the disturbance when it skipped.

Market on Tuesdays. The school was three kilometers down the road; he walked it every morning, sometimes running the last half-kilometer because he liked the burn in his legs, liked the way his mind went blank during the run. He was not the fastest. He was the most consistent. The teacher had noted this once, in a tone that was more observation than praise.

*You don't quit when it gets hard. You just go quiet.*

He had nodded and sat down.

He was always slightly outside things. Not unhappy — that was not the word for it. He could laugh, could sit in a pile of other children and be genuinely part of the noise. But there was a membrane between him and the full surrender of it. Some part of him always watching. Always noting exits.

He had told no one this. There was no language for it that didn't sound wrong.

---

There was a girl in the neighboring lane who could whistle through her fingers, a sound so sharp it scared the crows out of the trees. He had spent three weeks learning how. He never managed it, but he learned, in the process, how to be very patient with a thing that was failing him.

There was an old man — half-blind, moved like water around obstacles — who sat outside the grain seller's and played chess against himself. The boy had watched him for a month before asking to play. The man had beaten him in eleven moves. He came back the next day. And the day after.

The man never spoke during the game. Neither did he.

By the end of six months, the boy was winning. The man had laughed — a real laugh, surprised by itself — and said: *Most people want to learn to win faster. You learned to last longer.*

The boy had walked home thinking about that.

---

The evenings were the best part. The hills caught the light differently in the hour before dark — everything going amber and copper and long-shadowed. He would sit on the flat rock behind the house, the one that jutted out enough that you could see the valley below, and he would just look.

Not at anything specific. Just at the whole of it.

His father would sometimes come and sit next to him, and neither of them would say anything, and that was entirely fine.

Once, his father had put a hand on his shoulder and said: *Whatever happens, this is yours. You carry this.*

He had not known what his father meant.

---

The night came like all the others.

He ate. He helped wash the vessels. He lay down on the mat and listened to the village go quiet — the sounds tapering to dogs and insects and somewhere, a baby crying and then settling.

He closed his eyes.

He did not know it was the last night he would sleep like this — without listening for something wrong. He did not know that the quiet, which had always felt like safety, was a thing that could be taken.

He just slept.

And in the morning, the fire began.
