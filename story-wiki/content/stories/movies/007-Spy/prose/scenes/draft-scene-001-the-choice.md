# Scene 001 — The Choice

**Location:** University campus, Delhi. A corridor outside a lecture hall. Late afternoon — the light is going amber through institutional windows.
**Characters:** Alpha (19), Sarah (19)
**Purpose:** The moment Alpha decides to enlist. The last moment he and Sarah exist in the same world. Neither of them knows it yet.

---

The corridor was emptying. End of the last lecture before the winter break, and everyone had somewhere better to be. Alpha didn't move.

He was standing with his back against the wall when Sarah came out, still tucking notes into her bag. She almost walked past him.

"You're not going to the common room?" she said.

"No."

She stopped. He had that look — the one she'd learned to read over two years. Still face, but something behind the eyes doing a lot of work.

"What happened."

It wasn't a question. He held out a folded piece of paper. She took it and opened it, and her eyes moved across it once, and then she folded it closed and gave it back.

"It's not a request," she said.

"No."

"Rajan got one too?"

"Rajan and four others from our year. It's a general call. Anyone with training background who wants to volunteer gets priority placement." He paused. "I'm going to volunteer."

The corridor was almost empty now. Someone's laugh echoed from the far end, then faded.

"You don't have to," Sarah said. "Training background means they'll take the others first. You could—"

"I want to go."

She looked at him. He looked back at her, and for a moment neither of them said anything, and the space between them was full of all the things the last two years had been — the library arguments, the one evening on the roof of the economics building, the studied pretence that nothing had shifted between them.

"You want to go," she repeated. Not sarcastic. Trying to understand.

"Rajan is going. I'm not sending him in alone."

"Rajan isn't your responsibility."

"He's my friend."

"So is everyone in that call. That doesn't mean—"

"Sarah." His voice was flat but not hard. "I've decided."

She pressed her lips together. Looked at the folded paper in his hand. Looked back at him.

"When do you report?"

"Monday."

Four days. She nodded once, the slow nod of someone recalibrating.

"Okay," she said. Just that. She pulled her bag strap up onto her shoulder and turned away. Three steps down the corridor.

"Sarah."

She stopped. Didn't turn around.

"I'll write," he said.

She stood there for a moment. Then she kept walking.

*He didn't write. He told himself it was the war, the training, the weeks with no mail access. The truth was simpler and harder than that: writing would have meant admitting that leaving her in that corridor had been a choice, not a circumstance. He could not afford to know that about himself. Not yet. Not with what was coming.*

---

**Consequence:** The last civilian conversation of Alpha's life ends with a retreating back and a promise he will not keep. Sarah walks away believing he'll return. He walks away having already begun the arithmetic of erasure.
