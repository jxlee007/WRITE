# Scene 001 — Tathastu

**Location:** The edge of a mountain at sunrise. The tapasya is over. Three weeks of standing still in the cold while the wind tried to take him. The man is barely standing.
**Characters:** Vikrant (the first mortal — no name in legend, we give him one), Brahma
**Purpose:** The divine grant. The moment the entire dynasty's curse begins. Brahma is not pleased. He grants anyway. *Tathastu.*

---

The cold had stopped feeling like cold two weeks ago. It had become something else — a texture, like thick cloth, present everywhere. Vikrant was aware of it the way he was aware of his own heartbeat.

He heard the change before he saw it. A quality of light that was wrong — not wrong like a storm, but wrong like a lamp being turned on in a room that had never needed one. He kept his eyes closed.

"You can open them," said a voice. "I'm not that impressive."

He opened his eyes.

Brahma was sitting on a rock to his left, eating something from a small cloth — it looked like chikki. He did not look the way any of the scriptures suggested. He looked like a man in his sixties who had been interrupted in the middle of a walk, mildly inconvenienced, but trying to be polite about it.

He had four heads. Each one was doing something slightly different — one watching Vikrant, one apparently watching the sunrise, one looking vaguely irritated, and one asleep. The asleep one was snoring very quietly.

"Three weeks," Brahma said, to the head that was watching Vikrant. "That's longer than I expected from someone your age."

Vikrant's mouth was so dry it took him a moment to form words. "I wanted to be certain you'd notice."

"I noticed after four days. I was hoping you'd give up." He broke off a piece of chikki and chewed it. "What do you want?"

"Immortality."

Brahma stopped chewing. Then he resumed. He looked at the sunrise head, which seemed to look back at him, and something passed between the two faces that Vikrant couldn't interpret.

"That's what I thought," Brahma said. "Of course that's what it is. No one stands in the cold for three weeks asking for a good harvest. It's always something enormous." He looked back at Vikrant. "Why."

"I want to live long enough to understand everything."

"You won't."

"Then I want to live long enough to understand most things."

"You won't do that either."

"Then I want to try."

The irritated head shook very slightly. Brahma was quiet for a moment, turning the cloth over in his hands.

"I'm going to grant this," he said, "and you should know that I'm granting it with the full expectation that it will cause an enormous amount of trouble. Not for you, specifically. For everyone who comes after you." He looked at Vikrant directly. "Immortality does not arrive like a piece of paper with instructions. It evolves. From within. You understand what that means?"

"It must be earned—"

"Not earned. *Found.* Different thing. Earning implies a transaction. Finding implies that whatever you're looking for is already inside you — you simply haven't located it yet." He ate the last of the chikki. "Most people who receive this boon spend the rest of their very long lives mistaking the map for the territory. They teach their children the technique, and their children teach their children, and within three or four generations everyone is arguing about the map instead of looking for the territory." He paused. "This will happen with your family too. I want you to know that I know this, and I'm granting the boon anyway, because it is your right, and because your devotion is genuine, and because—" the irritated head and the watching head seemed to confer briefly — "occasionally I am curious about where a mistake of this scale leads."

He stood up.

"*Tathastu,*" he said.

The light changed.

When Vikrant looked down at his hands, they were the same hands they had always been. He felt the same. Exactly the same. He looked up.

Brahma was gone. There was a piece of chikki cloth on the rock.

*He stood there for a long time, waiting to feel immortal.*

*He didn't.*

*He went home. He cooked rice. He watched his wife sleep. He grew older, slowly — much more slowly than he should have — and the slower he aged, the more people came to ask him how he'd done it. He told them what Brahma had told him: it comes from within. It must be found. Not every person who hears this tries to find it. Most of them ask if there's a faster way.*

*There isn't. He told them that too. They didn't believe him.*

*Sixty years later, when his grandchildren's children were asking the same questions their great-great-grandparents had asked, he understood what Brahma had been curious about: not the immortality. The map. He wanted to see how many generations it would take before the map became the only thing anyone cared about, and the territory was completely forgotten.*

*The answer, as it turned out, was four.*

---

**Consequence:** The boon is granted. Vikrant does not understand its cost yet — and crucially, Brahma has deliberately left the conditions ambiguous. The phrase "evolves from within" is a divine loophole: it means only one person per generation can truly master the technique, and no one — not even Vikrant — can teach it as a rote process. The dynasty that will be built on this gift is already structurally flawed at the moment of its creation. Brahma knew. He granted anyway.
