# Scene 001 — Orange and the River

**Location:** The deep jungle. A river clearing. Morning.
**Characters:** Kiran (tiger cub, age: young enough to not know what he is yet), Maa (the orangutan — enormous, slow, smelling of bark and jungle flowers)
**Purpose:** The central relationship's first real test. Kiran has seen his reflection in the river for the first time and understood that he looks nothing like her. He has come to ask her about it. She will answer truthfully, because the alternative is worse.

---

He sat at the river's edge for a long time before she found him.

She had known where he'd gone — he always came to the river when something was wrong, on the grounds that moving water was easier to look at than standing still and thinking. She made her way through the undergrowth with the patience of someone who has spent a great deal of time being patient, and sat down on the rock behind him.

He was looking at the water. His reflection looked back.

"Maa," he said.

"Ji, beta."

"I look different from you."

A pause. She had known this conversation was coming — had known it since the day she'd found him, days-old and furious, abandoned in the sedge grass by the eastern reach. She had prepared for it the way you prepare for weather: you cannot stop it, but you can decide not to be surprised.

"You do," she said.

"I look like a tiger."

"You are a tiger."

He turned and looked at her. He was young enough that his stripes were still smudged, not yet crisp, and his eyes had the too-large quality of a face that hadn't grown into itself. He looked at her with the specific intensity of a child who has decided to believe what they're told but needs to hear it said correctly.

"But I'm your son," he said.

"You are," she said. Completely, simply.

"How."

She thought about this. Not the facts of it — those were clear enough — but the shape of an answer that a young tiger at a river's edge could carry.

"When I found you," she said, "you were very loud. And you were alone. And I picked you up." She shrugged one enormous shoulder. "After that you were mine."

"That's not how it works."

"How do you know how it works? You were very small."

He turned back to the river. His tail moved — not in play, but in the slow side-to-side of a mind working through something.

"The other tigers," he said.

"What about them?"

"They don't look at me like I'm a tiger. They look at me like I'm something wrong."

The river moved. A kingfisher landed on a branch over the water, regarded its own reflection, and departed.

"People," Maa said carefully, "look at things they don't understand the same way across all the forests. It's their limitation, not yours."

"But I *am* a tiger."

"Yes."

"So why—"

"Because you were raised by me." She looked at him steadily. "You walk differently. You pause in places they don't pause. You learned how to be careful before you learned how to be fast." She paused. "This confuses them. Tigers don't understand careful. Careful is mine — from the trees, from high places where a wrong step is permanent. I taught you what I know. It made you strange to them."

He was quiet.

"Does it bother you?" she said. "What they think?"

The tail again. Slower.

"It bothers me," he said, "that they're right about one thing. I'm not exactly a tiger." He looked at her. "And I'm not exactly an orangutan."

"No," she said.

"Then what am I?"

She reached over with one hand and put it on his head — enormous hand, orange fur against orange-and-black stripes. He didn't move away.

"You are Kiran," she said. "That's the only answer that holds water."

He looked back at the river. His reflection and hers sat side by side on the surface — large and small, fur and stripes, nothing matching.

"Okay," he said. Not satisfied, not settled. But willing to carry it.

She left her hand on his head a moment longer. Then took it back.

"Come," she said. "There are mangoes."

---

**Consequence:** Kiran accepts, for now, the answer that identity is name and nothing else. He is young enough that this holds. The test of it will come when his brothers arrive — when the people who share his actual face look at him and see the orangutan in his hesitations, in his carefulness, in the way he climbs before he charges. The question the story eventually asks is: is what Maa taught him a gift or a wound? The answer, as with most things she gave him, is both.
