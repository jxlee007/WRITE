# Scene 001 — The Seven Faces

**Location:** A government hospital waiting room, Nairobi. Fluorescent lights. Plastic chairs. The smell of antiseptic and old coffee. P7 (soldier, Kenyan military) has been brought in from a border incident. P1 (accountant, Indian-origin, works in Nairobi on an expat assignment) is waiting for news about a colleague who collapsed.
**Characters:** P1, P7
**Purpose:** Two of the seven encounter each other for the first time. They share a face. Neither of them knows about the others yet. The world's reaction to their shared face — and their own reaction to each other — launches the story.

---

The nurse stopped.

She had been walking with the kind of tired purpose that comes from a twelve-hour shift and three more hours to go, and she stopped in the middle of the corridor and looked between the two men sitting on opposite sides of the waiting room with an expression that was part confusion, part something closer to alarm.

Neither man had noticed the other yet.

P1 — Dev Mahajan, thirty-one, spreadsheets and expat salary and a talent for not standing out — was looking at his phone. P7 — Sergeant Major Kofi Adom, thirty-one, shoulders like a doorframe, the specific stillness of someone trained to conserve everything until it was needed — was looking at the ceiling. Neither was doing anything remarkable. They just happened to have the same face.

Not similar. The same.

The nurse looked between them once more, then went and got Dr. Patel, and Dr. Patel looked between them with the expression of someone encountering a math error they can't account for, and then Dr. Patel went and got nobody, because she didn't know who to get.

It was the movement of the hospital staff — the subtle rerouting, the too-deliberate casualness of people trying not to stare — that eventually made P7 lower his gaze from the ceiling.

He looked across the waiting room.

P1 looked up from his phone.

The silence that followed was very particular. Not awkward — too large for awkward. The kind of silence that arrives when a situation has no script.

P1 spoke first, because he was the kind of person who filled silences with words whether or not the words were adequate.

"That's—" he started.

"Yes," P7 said.

"You're—"

"I see it."

"We look—"

"Yes." P7's voice was not unkind. It was the voice of someone who had spent twenty years in situations where panic was a resource drain. "We look the same. I am looking at what you are looking at and I am having a similar reaction, which is that this is very unusual and I have no explanation for it."

Dev Mahajan, who had been paid a salary since age twenty-two to quantify things, tried to quantify this. "It's not just similar. It's—"

"Identical." P7 stood up. He was several inches taller, and much broader, and the face that sat on top of those shoulders was, in every feature that Dev's mother had photographed at his birth and every feature that Dev saw in mirrors every morning, the same face. "My name is Kofi."

"Dev."

Kofi held out a hand. Dev shook it, and that was strange too — the particular dissonance of shaking a hand attached to your own face.

"You're Indian," Kofi said.

"My parents are. I grew up in Pune. I'm here for work." Dev paused. "You're military."

"Kenyan Army."

A nurse walked past them, looked at both of their faces, and immediately found somewhere else to be.

"This is going to be a problem," Dev said, watching the nurse.

"It's already a problem," Kofi said. He sat back down, which was the signal that the conversation had moved from the phase of discovery into something more practical. "The question is what kind. There are two possibilities." He looked at Dev steadily. "Either there is an explanation — genetic, medical, scientific — and we find it. Or there is no explanation. In which case we find that instead."

"And then what?"

Kofi thought about this. He was a man who thought in outcomes, not feelings, which was why it took him a moment to find a word for what he was feeling, which was not fear but something adjacent to it — the sensation of a variable appearing in an equation that changes everything before you've had time to assess the change.

"I don't know," he said. Which was honest.

Dev looked at him for a moment. Then he looked back at his phone. "I'm going to google this."

"You won't find anything," Kofi said.

"I know. But I'm going to google it anyway, because I'm not a person who does well with nothing to do."

Kofi almost smiled. It looked very strange on a face that Dev was fairly accustomed to associating with his own expressions.

"Neither am I," Kofi said. "So we'll figure it out."

---

**Consequence:** Two of the seven have found each other by accident. What neither of them knows: five other people around the world woke up this morning with the same face, the same vague sense that something has changed, and an accumulating awareness that they are being *looked for* — by someone who knew about all seven of them long before any of them knew about each other.
