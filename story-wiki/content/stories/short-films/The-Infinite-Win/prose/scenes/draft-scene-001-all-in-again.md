# Scene 001 — All In — Again

**Location:** A harbour town. The ocean stretches out ahead — grey-green, enormous, visibly endless. An old man sits mending a net. A young man arrives with a boat, too small, too alone, with the specific expression of someone who has already decided.
**Characters:** Arjun (24 — has bet everything on this: his shop, his inheritance, his family's land), The Elder (73 — he tried the same ocean forty years ago and came back)
**Purpose:** Arjun encounters the only person in this town who has tried what he intends to try. He does not ask for advice. The Elder gives it anyway. Arjun ignores it. He goes anyway. The Elder watches him go, because he has been the Elder in this scene, and he knows how it ends — or thinks he does.

---

"You're going alone?"

The old man didn't look up from his net. He had seen the boat arrive and seen the single figure in it and done the math before asking the question.

"Yes," Arjun said.

"The legend says the ocean is infinite."

"I know the legend."

"It means you won't reach the bottom. By definition."

"I know what infinite means." Arjun tied the bowline with the precise, practiced motion of someone who has been preparing for this specific departure for a long time. "I'm going to conquer it anyway."

The Elder set his net down. He looked at the young man — at the quality of his certainty, which was total and specific and not stupid. This was not a reckless person. This was a person who had thought through everything except the thing that mattered.

"I went forty years ago," the Elder said.

Arjun stopped. "I know. The whole town knows."

"Then you know I came back."

"Yes."

"You're not curious why?"

Arjun looked at him for a moment. He had the face of someone with a great deal of patience for everything except the one thing he'd decided not to be patient about. "I am curious," he said carefully. "But I don't think you came back because the ocean beat you. I think you came back for other reasons. And my reasons for going are different from yours."

"What are your reasons?"

"The legend says whoever conquers the infinite ocean receives whatever they desire."

"Yes."

"I desire—" Arjun paused. Not for drama; for accuracy. "I desire to prove that a thing without a limit can still be met by a person without a limit."

The Elder looked at him for a long time. At the harbour. At the ocean.

"What did you bet?" he said.

"Everything."

"Your family's—"

"Everything." Simply. No defensiveness. Just the fact.

The Elder picked the net back up. Began working it again. "I bet everything too," he said, quietly. "I was twenty-nine. I had nothing to show for any of it." A long pause. "I came back because I had been at sea for thirty days and I hadn't reached the bottom, and I began to wonder if the thing I wanted — the thing I thought I was going to get when I conquered it — was actually the thing I wanted."

Arjun was quiet.

"Was it?" he said.

The Elder didn't answer immediately. The net moved through his hands with the ease of ten thousand repetitions.

"I wanted recognition," he said. "I wanted the town to see me the way I saw myself. I thought the ocean was the thing that would make them see it." He looked out. "Thirty days in, I realized I didn't care what the town thought anymore. I hadn't thought about them in weeks. I was just—" a small gesture — "there. Moving. Alive." He paused. "I came back because I'd gotten what I came for without finding the bottom. I didn't understand that at the time. I just thought I'd given up."

Arjun looked at the ocean.

"I won't come back early," he said.

"I know you won't." The Elder's voice was not unkind. "That's not the warning."

"What is?"

"The warning is that the thing you think you want at the bottom is probably not the thing you want. The ocean will show you what you actually want. And that might be harder to come home with than a victory."

Arjun untied the bowline from the dock.

"I'll find out," he said.

He pushed off.

The Elder watched him go — the small boat and the young man and the enormous grey-green water taking him. He watched until the boat was a speck and then a nothing.

"Yes," he said to the empty dock. "You will."

---

**Consequence:** Arjun goes. He will be at sea for a very long time — this is the first act's end, not its middle. The Elder will watch the harbour for thirty days, then sixty, then longer. Other people will write Arjun off — the bet was real, the shop is forfeit, the family land is gone. The Elder will not write him off. He does not know why, except that he recognises in Arjun's certainty something he lost in his own youth and has been trying to name ever since. The ocean is the only place it was ever fully present.
