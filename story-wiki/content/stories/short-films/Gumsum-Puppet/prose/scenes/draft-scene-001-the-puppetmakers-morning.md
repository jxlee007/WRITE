# Scene 001 — The Puppetmaker's Morning

**Location:** A large workshop on the upper floor of a narrow building in an old city. Morning. Light through wooden-slatted shutters — striped, warm, cutting across the room at shallow angles. The workshop is dense: tools hung with precision, wood shavings on the floor, bolts of cloth stacked in a corner, half-finished figures lining one shelf. At the window, in the good chair, finished and complete: the Puppet.
**Characters:** Anil (the maker — the only person in the room)
**Purpose:** We spend five minutes alone with Anil before the world intrudes. We understand, through his behaviour and his silences, exactly how far gone he is — without him saying a single word about it.

---

He came in carrying two cups.

He set one on the small table beside her chair and kept the other for himself. He stood at the window and drank it and looked out at the street below — the vegetable cart, the pigeons, the early light finding its first surfaces — and then he looked back at her.

She was where he'd left her the night before: slight forward lean, hands in her lap, face tilted toward the window. He had arranged her that way. He had spent eleven minutes on the angle of the head.

He set his empty cup on the workbench and picked up a brush.

The commission in the corner was three weeks past its delivery date. A corporate piece — a mascot figure for a confectionery company, fully mechanical, built to a spec sheet. He'd been working on it in the mornings, before the real work. He picked up the brush and held it over the mascot's face and did not apply it.

The morning light moved.

The Puppet's glass eyes, at a slightly different angle now, seemed to hold something different from what they'd held a moment ago. He had built this deliberately — three days of mathematics, the exact curvature of the iris, the depth of the pupil, so that as the light changed she appeared to shift expression. He had told himself it was a technical problem. The most interesting technical problem he had ever solved.

He set the brush down.

He crossed to the window and looked at her.

"Mehta called again," he said. His voice was level, conversational. "He wants the reveal at the end of the second act. He thinks the audience needs to be *told* you're a puppet or they won't believe it." A pause. "He's wrong. If they need to be told, we've failed before the telling."

The Puppet said nothing.

The cold tea sat on the small table beside her chair.

He reached out and straightened the cup — it had been slightly off-centre, half a centimetre at most, but off-centre. He straightened it. Then he moved her left hand a fraction — the way the morning light fell across the knuckles had been bothering him for twenty minutes, though he hadn't been able to name exactly what was wrong until now.

Better.

He went back to the workbench.

He worked on the mascot piece for forty minutes — real, steady work, the kind his hands knew how to do without his full attention. While he worked, he talked. Not continuously. In the way of someone who resumes a conversation that has been running for a long time.

"Four days," he said at one point.

And then later: "The rigging is right. The tension on the shoulder joint." He checked something on a schematic pinned to the wall. "I'll go through it tonight."

And then, very quietly, to himself or to her or to nothing: "You'll be seen."

He said it the way you say something that has been true for a long time and is still, for some reason, astonishing.

*He had built the eyes to shift with the light so that at 7 AM, when the sun came through the shutters at its lowest angle, the glass caught something that was indistinguishable — if you didn't know, if you were not the person who had spent three days calculating it — from warmth.*

*It was 7:04 AM.*

*He was not looking at her.*

*He didn't need to.*

---

**Consequence:** Anil works alone in his workshop with a puppet that cannot respond and a cup of tea that will stay cold until he remembers to pour it away. The performance is in four days. He is not nervous about the performance. He is protective of it — the way you protect something that will change once other people have seen it. Once she has been seen by a theatre full of strangers, she will no longer be only his. He knows this is coming. He has been preparing for it for eight months. He is not prepared.
