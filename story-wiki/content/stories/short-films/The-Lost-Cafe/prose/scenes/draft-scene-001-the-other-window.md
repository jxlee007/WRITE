# Scene 001 — The Other Window

**Location:** Arjun's flat, Bengaluru. 2:00 AM. He hasn't slept through the night in five years. The flat is clean because he cleans when he can't sleep — dishes, surfaces, everything put away. It looks like nobody lives here.
**Characters:** Arjun (35, five years in the same grief, hasn't moved furniture since), Arjun-2 (the same man, different choices, living in Pune — he is only present as a voice in Arjun's head at first, then something more)
**Purpose:** First contact. The wall between the realities begins to thin. Arjun hears something that isn't a memory and isn't a dream. He responds. The response is what makes it dangerous.

---

He was washing the same cup he'd already washed.

He knew he'd already washed it — it was clean when he picked it up — but his hands needed something to do at 2 AM and the cup was there. He stood at the sink and the water ran over it and he thought about nothing in the specific way you think about nothing when you are actively trying not to think about something.

The flat was very quiet.

And then it wasn't.

Not a sound, exactly. More like the quality of the silence changed — the way a room changes when someone enters it who hasn't made a sound yet. The presence of something that shouldn't be there, registered by nothing as precise as a sense.

He turned the tap off.

"Hello?" he said. Into his clean, empty flat, at 2 AM. Which meant he had finally lost his mind, or the five years had taken a toll he hadn't calculated, or—

*Don't.* A voice — his voice, exactly his voice, the frequency and the vowels and the particular way he slightly stressed the first syllable of words when he was trying to stay calm. *Don't answer. If you answer, it opens.*

He stood very still.

"What opens," he said.

A long pause. And then: *I don't know what to call it. I've been trying to not call it anything. I've been—* a small sound, not quite a laugh — *I've been very successful at ignoring this for three nights. I assumed it would stop.*

"It didn't stop."

*No.* Another pause. *You're in Bengaluru.*

"Yes."

*I'm in Pune.* Said the way someone says a fact they're offering as a handshake — look, here is verifiable information, I am trying to be reasonable about an unreasonable situation. *I live in Pune. I have for four years. I work at a design firm. I have—*

He stopped.

Arjun waited.

*I have a good life,* the other voice said. Very carefully. *I want to be clear about that. I'm not here — wherever here is — because something is wrong. Things are fine.*

"Things are fine," Arjun repeated.

*Yes.*

He stood at the sink in his clean empty flat and looked at the cup in his hand and thought about the last five years — the closed furniture, the washed dishes, the *not-sleeping* — and something caught in his chest.

"Good," he said. He meant it. He also meant its opposite. Both were entirely true.

*I know,* the other voice said. Quieter. *I know.*

The silence between them was its own kind of weight. Two versions of the same person at 2 AM, one of whom had made different choices and one of whom had not, recognising in the quality of the other's quiet what neither of them had said out loud.

"How long has this been happening?" Arjun said.

*Three nights. It starts at 2 AM. It ends — I don't know when it ends. I fall asleep eventually.* A pause. *You don't sleep.*

"Not through the night."

*No. I remember that.* He said it the way you say something about a shared past — which, in the most literal sense possible, it was. *I used to — I used to clean things when I couldn't sleep.*

Arjun looked at the cup.

"I still do," he said.

*I know,* the voice said again. And then: *I think we shouldn't keep talking. I think every time we talk it gets easier for this to happen, and I don't know where that leads.*

"Neither do I."

*So we should stop.*

"Yes," Arjun said.

They didn't stop for another forty minutes. They talked — carefully, warily, taking turns, the way you talk to someone you used to know who has become a stranger through the accumulation of different days — about small things: the city, the weather, a restaurant. Arjun did not ask about her. The other voice did not mention her. The not-mentioning was the loudest thing in the flat.

At 2:41 AM, the quality of the silence changed back.

He was alone.

He finished washing the cup he'd already washed, put it away, turned the tap off, and stood at the sink in the dark for a while.

*He isn't haunting you,* he thought. *You're haunting him.*

---

**Consequence:** The connection is now two-directional and active. Arjun knows it. The other Arjun knows it. What neither of them has said is that the other Arjun's life contains the woman — the specific person whose absence has held Arjun's life in suspension for five years — and she is, in that other reality, alive and fine and married to someone else. This information has not been spoken. It doesn't need to be. It's the shape of everything they didn't say.
