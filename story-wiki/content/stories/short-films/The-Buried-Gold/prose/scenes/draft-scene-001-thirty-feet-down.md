# Scene 001 — Thirty Feet Down

**Location:** An open field outside a village in Rajasthan. A hole in the ground, thirty feet deep. Meera is at the bottom of it.
**Characters:** Meera (52 — she has been digging this hole, on and off, for nineteen years), Old Champa (80, village woman, the only person who still comes to check on her)
**Purpose:** Meera is at the bottom of a literal hole. Old Champa arrives at the top. The argument they have every few months — the same argument — happens again. This time, something in it cracks.

---

"Meera!" The voice came from far above, thinned by distance and echoed by the walls of the shaft. "Meera, I've brought roti!"

Meera was scraping at the earth with a modified pickaxe — the third pickaxe, the other two having given up under the work — and she did not look up.

"I'm busy, Champa ji."

"You've been busy since 1996." Old Champa's voice was entirely without apology. "The roti will get cold. Come up."

"Lower it down."

"I'm eighty years old. I'm not lowering anything. Come up."

Meera stopped scraping. She looked at the walls of the shaft, at the particular layer of soil she'd reached today — redder than yesterday, which was promising, and also identical to what she'd thought was promising at twenty-six feet, and at twenty-two, and at seventeen. She looked up.

Old Champa's face appeared at the rim of the hole, small and brown and exasperated, sixty feet above her.

"How much longer?" Champa said.

"I'm close."

"You've been close since 2003."

"I am *closer* than I was in 2003."

"That's not the same as close."

Meera began climbing the ladder — the good ladder, the third one, pine rungs, reliable. "The original map says the gold is at a depth of forty feet. I'm at thirty. I have ten feet left."

"You've had ten feet left for seven years."

"The soil compresses differently than expected."

"Meera." Old Champa's voice had changed — not to anger, which Meera could handle, but to something softer, which she couldn't. "Sit and eat with me."

She reached the top and stood in the afternoon sun and felt, as she always did after time at the bottom, the specific shock of how much sky there was. Sixty feet of open air above her head. Her shadow was very small.

Champa had laid a cloth on the ground with two rotis and some achaar and a small pot of dal. She was already sitting, moving with the practiced patience of great age. Meera sat across from her.

They ate for a while in silence.

"Who gave you the map?" Champa said.

"My husband."

"Your husband who left."

"Yes."

"And where did he get it?"

"From his father. It's been in the family for four generations. The gold was buried by his great-grandfather during partition, to keep it safe during the crossing. He marked the spot and never made it back to collect it."

"And the spot is here? In this field?"

"That's what the map says."

Champa looked at the hole. "What if the map is wrong?"

Meera tore a piece of roti. "Then I'm wrong."

"And if you're wrong?"

"Then I've spent nineteen years in a hole."

Champa was quiet. A kite circled overhead, riding something warm.

"My daughter," Champa said — slowly, carefully, the way very old people introduce the thing they actually came to say — "is getting married in March. She has asked for you. She wants you to come."

"I'll be busy in March."

"Meera."

"I can't. I'm at thirty feet. I can't leave at thirty feet."

"You could leave for one day."

"I've left before. Things collapse. The water table shifts. I come back and lose two weeks of work."

Champa looked at her. At the hole. At her hands — Meera's hands, which were the hands of someone who had not stopped doing one particular thing for nineteen years. Strong. Scarred. Useful.

"You are not digging for gold," Champa said.

"Yes, I am."

"You are digging because your husband left and the gold was his reason for staying and you thought if you found it he would—"

"Don't," Meera said.

"He's not coming back, beta."

"I know that."

"Then—"

"I know that." Her voice was flat and certain. "I have known that for twelve years. I'm not looking for him. I'm looking for the gold."

Champa looked at her.

"And when you find it?" she said.

Meera tore another piece of roti and didn't answer.

*The truth was she hadn't thought beyond finding it. The gold was the boundary of the future — everything in her life had been arranged around the event of finding it, and she had no picture of what came after. Who she was after. Champa was wrong that she was waiting for her husband. But Champa was right about something she couldn't name yet: that the hole was a way of staying inside a single moment, where at least the direction was clear and the work was real and the effort could be measured in feet.*

*Thirty feet down was somewhere.*

*The surface, with its sky and its daughters getting married and its March, was everywhere. Everywhere was harder.*

"Come to the wedding," Champa said.

Meera finished the roti. She looked at the hole, at its precise dark mouth in the earth.

"I'll think about it," she said.

---

**Consequence:** She goes back into the hole after lunch. But she sits at the bottom for an hour without digging — just sitting in the dark, listening to the soil. This is the first hour in nineteen years she has sat in the hole without working. It is the story's first hint that she is beginning to hear something the digging has been drowning out.
